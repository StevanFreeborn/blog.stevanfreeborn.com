In the last few weeks, the tech industry has been hit by large layoff announcements from both Block and Atlassian. If you read the headlines or the executive memos, you would think we have officially entered the era where artificial intelligence is wholesale replacing human workers. We are seeing companies claim that AI tools have made them so efficient that they simply do not need as many people anymore.

My thought? The "AI" reasoning is currently somewhat of a loaded marketing excuse. As someone who spends their days in quality engineering and a large amount of their free time building software both with and without AI, I think it is worth asking: does this really make sense?

I am convinced that this narrative is largely a smokescreen. The reality is that the mechanics of these layoffs have very little to do with the magical efficiencies of AI and everything to do with poor financial performance and market pressure.

Let's take a peek at what is really happening with these layoffs as I see it.

## The "intelligence" cover story

When you take away the AI gloss of the executive announcements, you find a heavy reliance on keywords to justify these cuts.

Over at Block, Jack Dorsey announced they are reducing their organization by nearly half. His stated reason was not financial distress, and in fact, he claimed the business is strong and profitability is improving. Instead, he pointed to "intelligence tools" enabling a new way of working with smaller and flatter teams. I think in one statement I read they referred to this as being "intelligence-native". Whatever the fuck that means.

Similarly, Mike Cannon-Brookes at Atlassian framed their recent cuts around balancing the long-term success of the company with responsiveness to the world they operate in. The messaging is carefully crafted to make these reductions sound like a visionary pivot rather than a reactionary scramble.

## The reality in the numbers

The danger lies in letting the "AI" label obscure the current financial reality of these companies. The stock charts though tell a story that is much different than the CEO memos.

### Block's slide

Block's stock price has been struggling. In October 2025, it was trading at a high of $75.94, but by March 2026, it had slumped down to $59.77. When a company loses that much of its valuation, executives face immense pressure from Wall Street to aggressively cut costs. Blaming a 4,000-person layoff on AI is a convenient way to appease investors without admitting the company is struggling to maintain its market position or that management did fuck all to manage its hiring against the business's actual needs.

### Atlassian's nosedive

Atlassian's situation is even more stark. While they boasted $5.2 billion in revenue and $1.4 billion in free cash flow in their 2025 annual report, their stock price has absolutely cratered in recent months. In December 2025, the stock closed at $162.14. By early March 2026, it had plummeted to $73.84. That is about as close as you can get to a catastrophic drop. The executive management and board of directors wouldn't be doing their job or upholding their responsibility to shareholders if they didn't take some meaningful action. The issue though is trying to use AI as cover for it.

## The copycat effect

I believe that the market response to Block's announcement emboldened Atlassian to make a similar move. Block tested the waters by using AI as the scapegoat for a massive layoff. Once Atlassian saw that they could use the exact same playbook to soften the blow of their own financial difficulties and restructuring, they followed suit.

I think this looks like classic corporate strategy. Rather than admitting that they over-hired during the pandemic or failed to deliver the growth investors expected, leadership can simply point to the latest technological trend as the culprit. It shifts the blame from poor management to an inevitable technological shift. Remember when remote work was the scapegoat and everyone needed to implement RTO initiatives?

## Conclusion

I hope you will come away from reading this not with the idea that I am anti-AI. I am not. I firmly believe these tools can make us more productive. However, I also believe that attributing massive corporate layoffs to AI efficiencies is fundamentally flawed and dishonest. It is also hostile to the 1000s of employees who work at other companies whose management may see the positive response from the market and feel they have an incentive to do something similar at their shop.

Let's be clear: these are financial corrections. We need to stop weaponizing people's fear of AI job displacement and the investor hype around AI efficiency. Instead, we should hold leadership accountable for the true financial realities of their organizations.
